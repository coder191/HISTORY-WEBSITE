Origin







The dream of worldwide democrats and socialist nations was first picturised by Frederic Sorrieu in a painting in 1848; although nationalist struggles had started as early as 1789. A few features were highlighted by Sorrieu that lead to the evolution of the term, Nation State:
  • End of Absolutist Institutions.
  • A sense of Common Identity and a common history.
  • Liberty, Fraternity, Power of the People(Plebisite).
What is Nationalism & Liberty?

Nationalism is defined as a feeling of patriotism. Liberty(Obtained from the latin word liber; which means free) is defined as the freedom of an individual, equality before law, freedom of religion and freedom of speech.

Liberalism can be divided into 3 broad categories:

Political Liberalism
It stands for the end of autocracy and privelages by birth. Political liberalism emphasises on equality before the law, government by consent, a fixed constitution and universal suffarage.
In the ninteenth century it also stressed on inviolability of property.

Social Liberalism
It is defined as the freedom of lifestyle and equality of oppurtunity. A society where there exists no discrimination on grounds such as, racial, sexual, or religious.
Socially & Politically the landed aristrcracy was the dominant class on the continent. However, this group occupied a small percentage of the population. During the age of revolutions the educated upper middle class came into being as a result of the industrial revolution. It was this social group that led the revolution on social grounds.

Economic Liberalism
Laissez-Faire is the freedom of trade throughout a vast region, for the steady growth of the economy.
In early Europe there were innumerable obstacles to traders & businessmen:
  1. There were no uniform weights or measures; resulting in lengthy calculations.
  2. In some countries there were more than 30 types of currencies.
  3. A large number of tariff/tax barriers reduced the profit of traders.

The Age of Revolutions (1830-1848)







The liberal nationalist tried to free their respective countries from the rule of the conservatives and as a result they got themselves increasingly associated with revolutions in many regions. The liberal nationalists mainly consisted the literate middle class men. The first upheaval took place in France in July 1830. During the conservative regime the bourbon ruler who had been restored to power was overthrown. In his place, Louis Phillipe, a constitutional monarch was installed. Subsequently the revolt sparked an uprising in Brussels, which led to Belgium's Independence from the Kingdom of Netherlands.
After this event Austrian chancellor Duke Metternich made a remark that when France sneezes the rest of Europe catches cold. His remark on the situation was true due to the subsequent uprisings that took place in the rest of Europe.









The Chain Reaction





Following the French example; many other European nations renewed their struggle against monarchy and autonomy of power.
The very first uprising after the July Revolution of France took place in Brussels. It soon led to Belgium being liberated from The Kingdom of Netherlands; and later uprisings in:
  • Greece
  • Poland
  • Germany

The Greek War of Independence






Greece had been part of the Ottoman Empire since the fifteenth century.the growth of revolutionary nationalism in Europe sparked off a struggle for independence amongst the Greeks which began in 1821.
Nationalists in Greece got support from other Greeks living in exile and also from many west Europeans who had sympathies for ancient Greek culture. The treaty of Constantinople of 1832 recognized Greece as an independent nation.









Polish Revolution






After the Vienna Congress Poland was divided among the three great powers: Prussia, Austria and Russia. Since the Polish could not win in combat aginst the three powers it sought to a different form of rebellion; Romanticism.
Romanticism meant a movement which emphasised one past, one history, one culture. Patriotism among people was instilled by means of literature, folk music and dance.
After Russian occupation, the polish language was forced out of schools and the Russian language was imposed everywhere. In 1831,an armed rebellion against Russian rule took place which was ultimately suppressed. Many members of the clergy in Poland began to use the Polish Language at church gatherings and for all religious instruction; as a result a large number priests and bishops were put in jail or sent to Siberia by Russian authorities as a punishment. The use of polish became a symbol of struggle against Russian dominance.

Italian Unification






Italians were scattered over several dynastic states as well as the multi-national Habsburg empire. In the 19th century Italy was divided into seven states, of which ,only one Sardinia-Piedmont was ruled by an Italian princely house. The north was under Austrian Habsburgs, the center was under the pope and the south was under the bourbon kings of Spain. The failure of revolutionary uprisings in 1831 and 1848 resulted in the transferring of the responsibility of Italian unification to Victor Emanuel II, the ruler of Sardinia-Piedmont.
His Chief minister, Cavour led this unification process; he was neither a democrat nor a revolutionary, his major advantage was his brilliance at speaking French. Through his tactful diplomatic alliance with France he with the help of French forces defeated Austrian forces in 1859. A large number of armed volunteers under the leadership of Giuseppe Garibaldi also joined the fray. In 1860 they marched into south Italy and the Kingdom of Two Sicilies and succeed in winning the support of the local peasants in order to drive out the Spanish rulers. These wars and alliances led to the unification of Italy and victor Emanuel II was proclaimed as the king of united Italy. However the final step of Italian unification was completed after the battle of Sedan in 1871.

An area of tension after 1871 was the Balkan area. It comprises of modern day Romania, Bulgaria, Albania, Macedonia, Croatia, Montenegro, Serbia and Slovenia; their inhabitants were broadly known as 'Slavs.'
